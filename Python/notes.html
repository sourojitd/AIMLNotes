[cite_start]

Detailed Explanation:
These measure the "center" of your data[cite: 526].

    [cite_start]
  • Mean: The average of all values[cite: 537].
    • [cite_start]
  • Median: The middle value when all values are arranged in order[cite: 538].
    • [cite_start]
  • Mode: The most frequently occurring value[cite: 539].

üß† Kid Card: Allowance Averages

Your friends' allowances: $5, $6, $7, $8, $100. Mean (Average): $25.20. This is high because the $100 (an outlier) skewed it. Median (Middle): $7. This is a much better description of what a "typical" friend gets. Mode (Most Common): If two friends got $6, then $6 would be the mode.

4. Missing Values

[cite_start]

Detailed Explanation:
Missing values (often NaN) are an absence of data[cite: 563]. [cite_start]They can be handled by **Dropping** the rows/columns [cite: 571] [cite_start]or **Imputation** (filling them)[cite: 567]. [cite_start]Common imputation methods include filling with the Mean (average) [cite: 567][cite_start], Median (middle value, good for skewed data) [cite: 569][cite_start], or Mode (most common value, good for categorical data)[cite: 570].

üß† Kid Card: The 100-Piece Puzzle

You have a 100-piece puzzle, but 5 pieces are missing (NaN). What do you do? Dropping: Throw the whole puzzle away (bad!). Imputation: "Fill in" the hole. You could use the "average blue" of the sky (Mean), or just use another "grass" piece if the hole is in the grass (Mode).

[cite_start]

üß† Kid Card: That One Rich Friend

You ask 100 kids what their allowance is. 99 kids get $5-$10. One kid gets $500. That $500 is an **outlier**. A **Boxplot** is like a special fence that flags this $500 value as being "outside" the normal range.

1. Text Preprocessing

[cite_start]

Detailed Explanation:

[cite_start]Key tasks include: Lowercasing [cite: 595][cite_start], Removal of Special Characters [cite: 596][cite_start], Stopword Removal (common words like "and", "the", "is") [cite: 597][cite_start], and Stemming (reducing words to their root form, e.g., "running" -> "run")[cite: 600].

üß† Kid Card: Cleaning Up a Messy Sentence

A computer needs clean text. We take: "Wow! The BEST dogs are running!!"
Lowercase: "wow! the best dogs are running!!"
Remove Punctuation: "wow the best dogs are running"
Remove Stopwords: "wow best dogs running"
Stemming: "wow best dog run"
Now the computer sees the main idea.

2. Text Vectorization (Bag of Words)

[cite_start]

Detailed Explanation:
This means representing text in a numerical format[cite: 609]. [cite_start]The Bag of Words (BoW) model does this by counting the **frequency of each unique word**, ignoring grammar and word order[cite: 610, 611].

üß† Kid Card: Counting Words in a Bag

Computers read numbers, not words. BoW turns sentences into counts.
Sentence 1: "The cat sat." -> {The: 1, cat: 1, sat: 1, dog: 0}
Sentence 2: "The dog sat." -> {The: 1, cat: 0, sat: 1, dog: 1}
It's just a "bag" where you count the words. Order doesn't matter.

3. Sentiment Analysis

[cite_start]

Detailed Explanation:
This is the process of analyzing text to determine its emotional tone (positive, negative, or neutral)[cite: 612].

    [cite_start]
  • Lexicon-Based Approach: Uses a predefined dictionary ("lexicon") where each word has a sentiment score (e.g., "happy" = +1)[cite: 614, 615]. [cite_start]VADER is a popular tool for this, good for social media[cite: 616, 617].
    • [cite_start]
  • Machine Learning-Based Approach: A model is trained on labeled data (e.g., 1000 "positive" reviews, 1000 "negative" reviews) to learn the patterns of sentiment itself[cite: 618].

üß† Kid Card: The "Mood Reader"

Lexicon (Dictionary) Method: The computer has a dictionary: "love" = +2, "hate" = -2. It reads "I love this!" and sees a score of +2 (Positive!).

ML (Learning) Method: You *show* the computer 1,000 good reviews and 1,000 bad reviews. It *learns* the patterns itself, just like a spam filter learns what spam "looks" like.
